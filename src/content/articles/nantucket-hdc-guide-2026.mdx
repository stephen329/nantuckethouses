## What Is the HDC and Why Does It Matter?

The Historic District Commission is the single most powerful regulatory body on Nantucket. Every exterior alteration visible from a public way — from replacing a window to building a new home — requires HDC approval. In 2025, the commission reviewed over 400 applications, approving roughly 78% on the first pass.

> The HDC isn't just a hurdle — it's the reason Nantucket looks the way it does. Understanding their priorities is the difference between a six-week approval and a six-month ordeal.

If you're buying, building, or renovating on-island, the HDC will be part of your life. This guide gives you the insider knowledge to navigate the process efficiently.

## The Application Process Step by Step

### Pre-Application Consultation

Before you submit anything, schedule a pre-application consultation with HDC staff. This is free, informal, and — critically — not recorded in the public minutes. It's your chance to get honest feedback on whether your project will fly.

<Take>
I cannot overstate how important the pre-application meeting is. I've seen applicants skip this step and walk into a hearing with a $2M renovation plan that gets sent back to the drawing board. Thirty minutes with staff would have saved them three months. Every single one of my clients does this step first.
</Take>

### Submitting Your Application

Applications are accepted on a rolling basis, but hearings are scheduled monthly. The submission deadline is typically 15 business days before the hearing date. You'll need:

- **Completed application form** (available at the Town building department)
- **Site plan** showing the property boundaries, existing structures, and proposed changes
- **Architectural drawings** at a minimum of 1/4" scale
- **Material samples** for any new exterior finishes
- **Photographs** of existing conditions and neighboring properties

### The Hearing

HDC hearings are public meetings held in the first-floor meeting room at 2 Fairgrounds Road. Each application typically gets 10-15 minutes. The five commissioners will review your materials, ask questions, and vote.

## Design Standards That Actually Matter

### Materials

The HDC strongly favors traditional Nantucket materials: cedar shingles (left to weather naturally), white-painted wood trim, and wood or wood-clad windows. Vinyl and aluminum are almost universally rejected. Fiber cement (like HardiePlank) has gained acceptance for trim in recent years, but not as a primary siding material.

### Windows and Doors

Window proportions matter enormously. The commission expects true divided-light windows or high-quality simulated divided-light with spacer bars between the panes. The standard Nantucket window is a 6-over-6 double-hung, though other historical patterns are accepted depending on the architectural style.

<Take>
Here's the unwritten rule that trips up most off-island architects: the HDC cares about muntin bar width. If your SDL windows have thin, flat muntins, they'll get flagged. You want 7/8" putty-profile muntins — they cast shadows like real divided-light windows and the commissioners can tell the difference from the street.
</Take>

### Roofing

Cedar shingle roofs are the gold standard. Asphalt architectural shingles in weathered gray tones are accepted on most properties, but if you're in the core historic district (roughly Broad Street to India Street), cedar is expected. Standing-seam metal roofing has been approved for certain commercial and agricultural-style buildings.

## Cost Feasibility: What to Budget

A typical HDC-compliant renovation on Nantucket runs 15-25% higher than mainland construction costs, driven primarily by material requirements and the limited contractor pool. As of early 2026:

- **Cedar shingle siding**: $18-24 per square foot installed
- **Wood windows (true divided-light)**: $1,200-2,500 per window depending on size
- **Cedar roof**: $22-30 per square foot vs. $8-14 for architectural shingles

### Zoning Bonuses and Incentives

Properties in the Residential Old Historic (ROH) district may qualify for a 10% floor-area bonus if the renovation preserves original architectural features. Additionally, the Town's Community Preservation Act funds can offset costs for projects that restore historically significant structures.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Submitting without staff consultation** — as discussed above, this is the single biggest mistake
2. **Using mainland contractors unfamiliar with HDC standards** — they'll spec wrong materials
3. **Ignoring the neighbors** — the HDC takes neighborhood context seriously; an out-of-scale addition visible from the street will get denied
4. **Rushing the timeline** — build in at least one continuation; complex projects rarely pass on the first hearing

## Timeline: From Application to Approval

For a straightforward project (window replacements, minor exterior changes), expect 4-6 weeks from submission to approval. For new construction or major renovations, plan for 8-16 weeks, with at least one continuation hearing. Contested projects can stretch to 6+ months.

## Working with the HDC: A Strategic Approach

The commissioners are volunteers who care deeply about preserving Nantucket's character. Approach them as partners, not adversaries. Present your project as a contribution to the island's architectural heritage, and you'll find the process far smoother than its reputation suggests.
